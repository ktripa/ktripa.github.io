I am a Postdoctoral Researcher in the Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering at Texas A&M University, working with Professor Ashok Kumar Mishra. My research sits at the intersection of hydrology, climate science, and artificial intelligence—driven by a fundamental question: **how can we harness the synergy between physics-based hydrological models and modern machine learning to solve real-world water resource challenges?**

My core strength lies in bridging process-based understanding with data-driven approaches. While traditional hydrological models capture the physics of water movement and land-atmosphere interactions, machine learning offers unprecedented pattern recognition and predictive power. I develop **physics-guided deep learning frameworks** that combine the interpretability of physical models with the flexibility of AI, creating tools that are both accurate and mechanistically sound.

**What drives my research:**

My work is motivated by a simple reality—hydroclimatic extremes like flash droughts, compound heatwaves, and floods are intensifying under climate change, threatening water security, agriculture, and ecosystems. I am passionate about developing **actionable early warning systems** that can help communities, farmers, and water managers prepare for these events. This isn't just about publishing papers; it's about creating tools that work in the real world.

Currently, I lead an NSF-funded project developing **FlashCAT**—an open-source Python toolkit for flash drought identification and prediction that's being adopted by USDA researchers and agricultural stakeholders. I collaborate with NASA scientists on soil moisture memory research, and work directly with Texas farming communities to translate academic findings into operational decision-support systems.

**My research philosophy:**

I believe the best science happens at disciplinary boundaries. My approach combines rigorous physical understanding (land surface models, soil moisture dynamics, land-atmosphere coupling) with cutting-edge computational methods (transformers, explainable AI, physics-informed neural networks). I invest deeply in reproducible, open-source research—every model I develop comes with documented code, comprehensive testing, and clear examples so others can build on this work.

**Looking ahead:**

I am actively seeking postdoctoral fellowship opportunities and faculty positions where I can continue pushing the boundaries of hydroclimatic prediction while mentoring the next generation of computational hydrologists. If you're working on problems at the intersection of water, climate, and AI, I'd love to connect.
